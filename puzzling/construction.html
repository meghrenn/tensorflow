Construction

"Having decided upon the location of its permanent home, the university now devoted all its energies to ready it for occupancy by the fall of 1970" (Holmes, 1972, p. 118).

"In his development plan, Arthur had written: "Buildings must grow out of the ground, clustered with other buildings or trees, but never sit blatantly on top of the ground. Forms must be simple and geometrically concise, as elaborate forms and fussy details show weakness... Just as the prairie landscape has been reduced to essentials, so must its buildings be elemental, of the earth" (Stouck, 2013, p. 237).

"After considering several alternative approaches, it was decided to construct the north wing of the academic area, followed by the first stage of the recreation complex which would be used for various functions.

"A major portion of the north parking area would be developed in this phase, however, the extreme northwest corner of the parking area is to be utilized for siting of the "temporary" buildings presently located on the junior college campus ("Landscape Analysis...", n.d., p. 24).

"...the building took its destined shape, with its great horizontal beam structure and narrow fenestration something akin to a fortressed medieval city" (Stouck, 2013, p. 236).

"[I]t is questionable whether the coulee areas will endure the high intensity use generated by the concentrated academic and living areas. Under the pressure of university life, students predictably react vigorously during their moments of outdoor leisure. The use of artificial restraints cannot fully protect against such abuse and very possibly these restraints could lead to rebelliously intentional misuse. Today's students often react with hostility towards restrictions that appear to them arbitrary and artificial.

"Taking a less cryptic view, it is easy to imagine informal toboggan runs appearing along these fragile slopes in mid-winter, followed by cardboard sliding in the summer. Because of the nature of the site, such considerations cannot be passed off as merely disciplinary problems" ("Landscape Analysis...", n.d., p. 24).

"...his supreme achievement.... a single nine-storey building, 912 feet long, in which there was no spatial differentiation between the humanities and the sciences and where faculty offices and student rooms were the same size" (Stouck, 2013, p. 238).

"...a dialogue between history and utopia" (ibid, p. 239).
